DIABETES DIETARY GUIDELINES — Based on American Diabetes Association Standards

CARBOHYDRATES AND BLOOD SUGAR:
Carbohydrates directly raise blood glucose levels. People with diabetes should monitor total carbohydrate intake per meal. Recommended carbohydrate intake is typically 45-60 grams per meal depending on individual needs. Foods with a high glycemic index (above 70) cause rapid blood sugar spikes and should be limited. Low GI foods (below 55) are preferred as they cause slower, more gradual rises in blood sugar.

SUGAR AND SWEETS:
Added sugars should be minimized. Ice cream, cakes, cookies, and sweetened beverages are high in sugar and can cause rapid blood glucose spikes. If consumed, they should be in very small portions and accounted for in the total carbohydrate budget. Sugar-free alternatives are available but should still be consumed in moderation.

FIBER:
Dietary fiber slows glucose absorption and helps control blood sugar. Target at least 25-30 grams of fiber per day. High-fiber foods include vegetables, legumes, whole grains, and fruits. Foods with more than 5 grams of fiber per serving are considered high fiber and are beneficial for diabetics.

FATS:
Saturated fats should be limited to less than 10% of total calories. Choose healthy unsaturated fats from sources like olive oil, avocado, nuts, and fatty fish. Trans fats should be completely avoided as they worsen insulin resistance.

SODIUM:
People with diabetes are at higher risk of hypertension. Sodium intake should be limited to less than 2300mg per day. Processed foods, canned goods, and fast food are typically high in sodium.

PROTEIN:
Protein has minimal effect on blood glucose. Lean protein sources such as chicken, fish, legumes, and tofu are recommended. For diabetics with kidney complications, protein intake may need to be further restricted.

MEAL TIMING:
Eating regular meals at consistent times helps stabilize blood sugar. Skipping meals can lead to hypoglycemia. Small frequent meals are often better tolerated than large infrequent ones.
